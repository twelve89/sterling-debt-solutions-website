Consumer Rights

Last Updated: January 1, 2026

Sterling Debt Solutions is committed to conducting our business with transparency, fairness, and full respect for the rights of every consumer. This page summarizes some of the key rights you have under federal law when dealing with a debt collector. This summary is provided for general information only and is not a substitute for the full text of the law or for legal advice.

Your Rights Under the FDCPA

The federal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), 15 U.S.C. § 1692 et seq., sets out the rules that third-party debt collectors must follow when attempting to collect a debt. Among other things, the FDCPA gives you the right to:

  • Be treated fairly, without harassment, abuse, or oppressive conduct
  • Be free from false, deceptive, or misleading representations
  • Not be contacted at unusual or inconvenient times (generally before 8:00 AM or after 9:00 PM your local time) unless you agree
  • Request that a collector stop contacting you at your place of employment if your employer prohibits such communications
  • Be told the name of the creditor to whom the debt is owed and the amount of the debt
  • Dispute the debt in writing and request validation
  • Ask the collector to cease further communication

Debt Validation

Within five days after a debt collector first contacts you about a debt, the collector must provide you with a written notice (sometimes called a "validation notice") that includes:

  • The amount of the debt
  • The name of the creditor to whom the debt is owed
  • A statement that, unless you dispute the debt within 30 days, the debt will be assumed to be valid
  • A statement that if you notify the collector in writing within 30 days that you dispute the debt, the collector will obtain verification of the debt and mail it to you
  • A statement that, upon your written request within 30 days, the collector will provide the name and address of the original creditor if different from the current creditor

How to Dispute a Debt

If you believe a debt is not yours, that the amount is incorrect, or that you have already paid or settled the account, you have the right to dispute the debt. To submit a dispute:

  1. Send your dispute in writing to Sterling Debt Solutions at the address listed below.
  2. Include your name, the account reference number (if known), and a clear explanation of the reason for your dispute.
  3. If you have supporting documents — such as proof of payment, identity theft reports, or prior correspondence — include copies (not originals).
Important: Once we receive your written dispute, we will cease collection activity on the disputed portion of the account until we have obtained verification and mailed it to you.

How to Stop Communications

You may notify Sterling Debt Solutions in writing that you want us to stop contacting you about a debt. Once we receive such a written request, we may contact you only to confirm that no further communication will occur or to notify you of a specific action we intend to take, as permitted by law.

Requesting that we stop communicating with you does not eliminate the debt and does not prevent a creditor or collector from pursuing other legal remedies that may be available.

Your Rights Under the FCRA

The federal Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), 15 U.S.C. § 1681 et seq., governs how information about consumers is collected, used, and shared by credit reporting agencies and furnishers of information (including debt collectors). Under the FCRA, you have the right to:

  • Know what is in your credit file by requesting a credit report from a consumer reporting agency
  • Dispute inaccurate or incomplete information and have it corrected or removed
  • Be told if information in your file has been used against you in connection with credit, insurance, employment, or another transaction
  • Limit "prescreened" offers of credit and insurance you receive
  • Seek damages from violators

If you believe information we have reported about you to a credit reporting agency is inaccurate, please contact us directly so we can investigate.

Filing a Complaint

If you believe Sterling Debt Solutions has violated the FDCPA, the FCRA, or any other applicable law, you may file a complaint with:

  • Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) — consumerfinance.gov/complaint
  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • Your state attorney general's office
  • Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs — see our State Disclosures page

You may also have the right to sue a debt collector in state or federal court for damages. If you prevail, you may be awarded damages, attorneys' fees, and court costs. Nothing on this page constitutes legal advice, and you may wish to consult an attorney about your specific situation.
